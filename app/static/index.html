How Much to Take Help?

The recommended daily dose of CBD varies based on several factors, including the individual's body weight, the condition being treated, the individual's tolerance, and the concentration of CBD in the product. Always consult with a healthcare professional when determining the right dosage.

  1. Start Low and Go Slow: It's a common recommendation to start with a low dose and gradually increase until you find the right amount that works for you. This is because everyone's body reacts differently to CBD.
  2. General Health and Wellness: For general health and wellness, many people start with a daily dose of 5-20 mg.
  3. General Anxiety: Some people might take smaller doses daily (like 10-50 mg) and then adjust based on personal experience and symptoms.
  4. Other Forms of Anxiety: There are limited studies on conditions like post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) or anxiety-induced insomnia. However, some individuals report relief at doses as low as 25 mg.
  5. Chronic Pain: Some studies have used doses of 20-1,500 mg per day for chronic pain.
  6. Sleep Disorders: Between 40-160 mg might be used to address sleep disorders.
  7. Epilepsy: Clinical trials have prescribed 200-300 mg daily.
  8. Glaucoma: Some sources suggest not exceeding 40 mg daily to increase intraocular pressure.

Lastly, it's crucial to consider it as part of a comprehensive treatment plan and not a standalone solution. Always consult with a healthcare professional or therapist familiar with CBD before starting or adjusting a regimen.

The Evolution of Cannabidiol (CBD): From Stigma to Mainstream Acceptance


Context:

Cannabidiol (CBD), a compound derived from the cannabis plant, has a history that is as controversial as it is intriguing. Cannabis, recognized for its therapeutic properties in ancient civilizations, later faced a wave of prohibition in the 20th century due to concerns surrounding its psychoactive properties (mainly attributed to THC). In recent decades, CBD has gained significant attention for its potential health benefits, distinguishing itself from the more infamous THC.


Timeline of Events:

  1. 2737 BCE: Ancient Chinese texts, including the Shennong Bencaojing, mention the therapeutic use of cannabis for ailments such as rheumatism and malaria (Russo, 2007).
  2. 1830s: Irish physician William O'Shaughnessy introduces cannabis to Western medicine after observing its use in India. He documented its potential as an analgesic and muscle relaxant (O'Shaughnessy, 1843).
  3. 1940: CBD is isolated and identified by Roger Adams and his team at the University of Illinois (Adams et al., 1940). However, its structure and configuration remained unclear until 1963.
  4. 1963-1964: Dr. Raphael Mechoulam at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem elucidates the stereochemistry of CBD and, shortly after, THC, distinguishing the non-psychoactive properties of CBD from the psychoactive properties of THC (Mechoulam & Shvo, 1963).
  5. 1970: The Controlled Substances Act in the United States classifies cannabis, including CBD, as a Schedule I substance, making its research and use highly restricted (Drug Enforcement Administration, 1970).
  6. 1980s: Dr. Mechoulam's further research uncovers potential therapeutic benefits of CBD for epilepsy (Cunha et al., 1980).
  7. 1990s: Discovery of the endocannabinoid system sheds light on how cannabinoids, including CBD, interact with the human body (Devane et al., 1992).
  8. 2013: The story of Charlotte Figi, a young girl with Dravet Syndrome, gains public attention. The use of a high-CBD cannabis strain significantly reduced her seizures, sparking a surge in interest and acceptance of CBD for therapeutic purposes (Maa & Figi, 2014).
  9. 2018: The US Farm Bill is signed into law, effectively legalizing the production of hemp (a form of cannabis with low THC content) and the extraction of CBD from it. This marked a significant shift in the public and legal perception of CBD (U.S. Congress, 2018).
  10. 2020: Multiple clinical trials and reviews indicate CBD's potential for treating conditions like anxiety, chronic pain, and certain types of epilepsy. The FDA approves Epidiolex, a CBD-based drug, for the treatment of seizures associated with two rare forms of epilepsy (FDA, 2020).

Present Day:

CBD products, ranging from oils to edibles, have found their way into mainstream markets around the world. With ongoing research and an evolving regulatory landscape, CBD's role in healthcare and wellness continues to expand.

Typical Use Cases


CBD Oil:

Typical Use Cases:

  • Sublingual use (under the tongue)
  • Added to food or drinks
  • Used topically for skincare or localized pain

Pros:

  • Versatility: Can be consumed in multiple ways.
  • Precise Dosing: Easier to measure exact dosages with droppers.
  • Rapid Absorption: When used sublingually, it can be absorbed rapidly.

Cons:

  • Taste: Some people find the natural taste of CBD oil to be unpalatable.
  • Variable Absorption: Bioavailability can vary based on how it's consumed.

CBD Gummies:

Typical Use Cases:

  • Easy and discrete consumption
  • Consistent dosing

Pros:

  • Taste and Convenience: Often flavorful and easier for some to consume.
  • Consistent Dosing: Each gummy has a fixed amount of CBD.
  • Longer-lasting: Digestive system processing can lead to prolonged effects.

Cons:

  • Delayed Onset: Takes longer to feel effects due to digestion.
  • Lower Bioavailability: Less CBD is absorbed compared to other methods.
  • Added Ingredients: Some gummies contain sugars or other additives.

Vaping CBD:

Typical Use Cases:

  • Immediate relief
  • Respiratory route

Pros:

  • Rapid Onset: Effects can be felt almost immediately.
  • Higher Bioavailability: More CBD is absorbed via the lungs compared to other methods.
  • Adjustable Dosing: Users can take as many or as few puffs as needed.

Cons:

  • Potential Risks: Vaping anything can have potential respiratory risks.
  • Device Maintenance: Requires a vaporizer or e-cigarette device.
  • Shorter Duration: Effects might not last as long as edibles.

Combining Types:

There might be scenarios where combining different types of CBD products can be beneficial. For example:

  • Using CBD oil for a consistent baseline throughout the day and vaping for immediate relief during sudden episodes of anxiety.
  • Combining gummies for a longer-lasting effect with topical CBD oil for localized pain or skin issues.

Pros:

  • Tailored Effects: Combining allows for a more customized CBD experience.
  • Prolonged Relief: Combining can lead to both immediate and long-lasting relief.

Cons:

  • Dosage Management: Users need to be cautious not to overconsume.
  • Cost: Buying multiple product types can be more expensive.

Remember, everyone's body reacts differently to CBD, so what works for one person might not work for another. It's essential to start with a lower dose and consult with a healthcare professional before starting or combining CBD products.

How does CBD Help?

CBD oil is thought to help in several ways:

  1. Serotonin Receptor Modulation: CBD is believed to interact with serotonin receptors in the brain, which can help regulate mood and behavior. Serotonin is often termed the “feel-good” neurotransmitter because it contributes to feelings of well-being and happiness.
  2. Endocannabinoid System (ECS): CBD interacts with the endocannabinoid system, which plays a crucial role in regulating various physiological processes, including mood, stress, and anxiety.
  3. Neural Regeneration: Chronic anxiety may damage neural circuits in the brain, and there's evidence to suggest that CBD can facilitate the regeneration of neurons in certain areas.
  4. Anti-Inflammatory Effects: CBD has anti-inflammatory properties, and since inflammation can lead to mood disorders like anxiety and depression, reducing inflammation may help alleviate anxiety symptoms.
  5. Anxiolytic Effects: Some research suggests that CBD may possess anxiolytic effects that reduce anxiety, although the exact mechanisms are still under investigation.

Remember that individual responses to CBD can vary greatly, and not everyone may experience relief from anxiety with its use. The research on CBD is still in its early stages, and there isn't a consensus among scientists and doctors about its effectiveness and safety in the long term. Always consult a healthcare provider before using CBD or any other supplement, especially if you are pregnant, nursing, have a medical condition, or are taking any medications.

General CBD Advice

Advice for Someone Starting Out with CBD Products:

  1. Consult with a Healthcare Professional: Before starting any CBD regimen, consult with a healthcare professional, especially if you have any pre-existing medical conditions or are taking medications. They can provide personalized guidance and ensure there are no potential interactions with your current treatments.
  2. Understand CBD and Its Variants: CBD comes in various forms, including oils, tinctures, capsules, edibles, topicals, and more. Each form has its unique properties and absorption rates. Understand the differences and choose the one that suits your needs and preferences.
  3. Start with a Low Dosage: If you're new to CBD, start with a low dosage and gradually increase it as needed. Everyone's body reacts differently, so it's essential to find the right dosage for your specific needs. You can typically find dosage recommendations on product labels or consult with a healthcare professional.
  4. Read Product Labels: When purchasing CBD products, read the labels carefully. Look for information on the CBD content, dosage, ingredients, and any third-party testing results. Reputable brands provide this information to ensure transparency and product quality.
  5. Choose High-Quality Products: Opt for CBD products from reputable brands that use high-quality, organically grown hemp and follow good manufacturing practices (GMP). Research the brand's reputation and read customer reviews.
  6. Be Patient: CBD's effects may take time to become noticeable. It's not uncommon for people to use CBD for a few weeks before experiencing significant benefits. Be patient and consistent with your usage.
  7. Stay Informed: Keep up to date with the latest research on CBD and its potential benefits. The field of CBD research is continually evolving, and new findings may provide insights into its uses and effects.
  8. Be Mindful of Side Effects: While CBD is generally considered safe, some people may experience side effects such as dizziness, dry mouth, changes in appetite, or diarrhea. If you experience adverse effects, consider adjusting your dosage or discontinuing use.
  9. Know the Legal Status: Understand the legal status of CBD in your region or country. Laws regarding CBD can vary significantly, so it's essential to stay compliant with local regulations.
  10. Avoid Unsubstantiated Claims: Be cautious of products or companies that make grandiose health claims about CBD. While CBD has shown promise in various studies, it's not a miracle cure for all ailments. Be skeptical of claims that seem too good to be true.
  11. Consider Full-Spectrum vs. Isolate: CBD products can be full-spectrum (containing other cannabinoids and compounds from the hemp plant) or CBD isolate (pure CBD). Some people prefer one over the other, so consider your preferences and potential benefits.
  12. Keep a Journal: To track the effects of CBD, consider keeping a journal. Note the dosage, time of day, and any changes in your symptoms or well-being. This can help you adjust your usage for optimal results.

Remember that while CBD shows promise for various conditions, it's not a replacement for medical treatment when needed. Always consult with a healthcare professional for serious health concerns or conditions. CBD is a complementary wellness tool that can be integrated into a healthy lifestyle.
